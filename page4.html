Impressionism (1860 – 1920)

  • When people look at Impressionist paintings like those by Degas or Renoir, they notice loose brushstrokes and vibrant colors.
  • The figures often appear in natural settings, capturing fleeting moments of everyday life.
  • The scene often feels diagonal and energetic rather than balanced and calm.
  • Faces show big, clear emotions, and textures such as skin, fabric, and metal look rich and real.
  • All of this makes the work feel intense and theatrical — a key sign of Impressionist art.
Edgar Degas
Swaying Dancer (Dancer in Green)
Degas captures a fleeting rehearsal moment with delicate brushwork and a focus on movement and posture, highlighting the everyday immediacy central to Impressionism.
Pierre-Auguste Renoir
The Large Bathers
Renoir's warm palette and soft handling of light emphasize sensual color and the pleasure of outdoor leisure, characteristic of Impressionist painting.
Berthe Morisot
Young Girl in a Park
Morisot's intimate scene uses soft brushwork and subtle color shifts to convey atmosphere and the quiet presence of everyday life.

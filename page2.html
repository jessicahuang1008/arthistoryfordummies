Rococo (1730 – 1770)

  • Palace luxury, curling gold details, and rooms that feel over decorated but elegant
  • Soft pastel colors everywhere (think Sophia Coppola's 2006 Marie Antoinette movie)
  • Romantic, floral settings with gardens, ribbons, and nature
  • Flirty, playful moments with couples lounging, chatting, or simply up to no good
  • Light, sweet, and decorative overall, like sweet dessert and that makes Rococo very easy to spot
Jean-Honoré Fragonard
The Music Lesson
In this painting by Fragonard, everything looks soft, pastel, and carefree, which is why it reads Rococo right away. The scene is about flirting in a dreamy garden, not anything serious — flowing clothes, rosy faces, greenery, and gentle light make it feel romantic, fluffy, and unserious. Instead of drama or realism, the painting focuses on beauty, charm, and pleasure, which is exactly the visual vibe Rococo is known for.
François Boucher
Portrait of Madame de Pompadour
This painting screams Rococo: almost overly decadent yet still romantic and elegant. Soft pastel colors, silky fabrics, flowers, and a relaxed, luxurious setting make the scene feel playful, refined, and focused on beauty and pleasure — all key visual traits of Rococo art.
Elisabeth Vigée Le Brun
Marie-Antoinette in a Rose Dress
This portrait of Marie‑Antoinette feels Rococo immediately through its soft pastel palette, glowing skin, and feathery textures that make everything look light and elegant. The relaxed pose, fresh roses, and airy outdoor setting create a dreamy, decorative world focused on beauty, pleasure, and refinement. The painting evokes charm, luxury, and aristocratic fantasy.

Rococo (1730 – 1770)

  • Think palace luxury, curling gold details, and rooms that feel over decorated but elegant
  • Soft pastel colors everywhere, like a clash of pastel and the dreamy look of Marie Antoinette
  • Romantic, floral settings with gardens, ribbons, and nature woven into the scene
  • Flirty, playful moments with couples lounging, chatting, or showing off silky fashion
  • Light, sweet, and decorative overall, more like dessert than a serious dramatic scene, which makes Rococo easy to spot
Jean-Honoré Fragonard
The Music Lesson
The Musical Contest is basically an 18th-century flirt-off in a fancy garden. Two guys are trying to impress a woman by making music, not to save the world, just to win her attention. Everything feels light, romantic, and a little playful, from the soft pastel colors to the lush greenery and elegant outfits. No drama, no deep tragedy, just charm, beauty, and good vibes. That focus on love, leisure, nature, and decoration over serious subjects is exactly what makes this painting so typically Rococo.
François Boucher
Portrait of Madame de Pompadour
Madame de Pompadour by François Boucher looks Rococo because everything feels light, decorative, and elegant rather than serious or dramatic. Soft pastel colors, silky fabrics, flowers, curved shapes, and a relaxed, luxurious setting make the scene feel playful, refined, and focused on beauty and pleasure — all key visual traits of Rococo art.
Elisabeth Vigée Le Brun
Marie-Antoinette in a Rose Dress
This portrait of Marie-Antoinette by Elisabeth Vigée Le Brun shows key Rococo features: soft pastel colors, delicate details, and a light, playful mood. The elegant pose, floral background, and focus on beauty and luxury are typical of Rococo art, which often celebrated aristocratic life and romantic themes with a sense of whimsy and charm.

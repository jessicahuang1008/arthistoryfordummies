Neoclassicism (1750 – 1800)

  • First impression is order and control, nothing feels messy, dramatic, or out of place
  • People pose calmly like statues, with clear outlines and smooth skin, not messy movement or wild emotion
  • The scenes are usually historical or mythological, with a focus on virtue and moral lessons
  • Colors are more muted and restrained compared to Baroque paintings.
  • Overall feels like ancient Greece and Rome got a makeover and came back as disciplined art
Jean Auguste Dominique Ingres
Joséphine-Éléonore-Marie-Pauline de Galard de Brassac de Béarn, Princesse de Broglie
Princess de Broglie by Jean Auguste Dominique Ingres shows key Baroque features: strong contrast between light and dark, a violent scene shown at its most intense moment, and figures that look solid and physically engaged. The tight framing, visible effort in the bodies, and clear emotion create drama and realism that are typical of Baroque painting.
Jacques-Louis David
Napoleon Crossing the Alps
Napoleon Crossing the Alps by Jacques-Louis David shows key Neoclassical features: calm, heroic poses, clear outlines, and a sense of order and dignity. The painting is formal and idealized, showing Napoleon as a powerful, noble figure in a grand historical moment — typical of Neoclassical art's focus on virtue and moral lessons.

The Oath of the Horatii
The Oath of the Horatii by Jacques-Louis David shows key Neoclassical features: calm, heroic poses, clear outlines, and a sense of order and dignity.
